## What is Entropy?

Today entropy is often used as an umbrella term to describe natural disorder, chaos, and randomness that occurs in the universe, but its inventor, Rudolf Clausius, initially created it as a way to measure this disorder in thermodynamics. It was also used to provide a stable definition for the 2nd Law of Thermodynamics, which states that the total entropy of the universe is always increasing. Entropy fundamentally describes the tendency of energy to disperse: the way that hot objects get cold and cold objects get warm is one classical way of highlighting this energy diffusion.

There are multiple types of entropy: Shannon entropy, Boltzmann entropy, fine grained entropy, and coarse grained entropy to name a few. Shannon entropy is also called information entropy because it relates to how the amount of information we can know about a system correlates to its entropy, specifically that they're inversely correlated: the higher the entropy the less information we know. Boltzmann entropy relates to the number of microstates in a system: the more microstates the higher the entropy. The number of states is directly influenced by things like increases in volume, pressure, or temperature in the system. For fine and coarse grained entropy, they derive their names from fine and coarse sand. Fine grained entropy is the measurement of every property of every particle, or the microscopic view that provides precision. However this is typically difficult to measure so scientists often utilize coarse grained entropy where the averages of a system are measured, or the macroscopic view.
