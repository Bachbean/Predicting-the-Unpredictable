https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xE7qO6OvKDY: (Chaos Theory Explained in Simple Terms)
This video discusses the basic ideas and foundations of chaos theory and deterministic systems, which are systems in which we use initial conditions and something's current state to accurately predict future states. Chaos theory explores the idea of how within deterministic systems, errors arising from measuring the initial conditions and current state can compound and lead to something that appears as randomness. The classic example is typically characterized by the hypothetical scenario in which a butterfly in Mexico flapping its wings could lead to the formation of a tornado in Texas. 

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VVQY5NNbM3k: (What is Chaos theory? & How its Used Today)
This video defines chaos theory as the study of predicting inherently unpredictable systems, and chaos as the "unpredictibility in the behavior of a complex natural system." Although chaos theory began as a method used to find patterns in the weather and meteorology, its application in a wide range of fields has led to numerous discoveries. For example the walking motion of some biped robots was found to follow a chaotic dynamic, and the process in which some molecules bind when forming synthetic polymers was optimized when chaos theory was applied. 

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5fRhasVmcUE: (Chaos Theory and The Butterfly Effect - Predicting The Unpredictable)
Really great video which clarifies most of the general questions I had about chaos theory and presented by knowledgeable researchers on the topic. "Chaos" and "random" are often used interchangeably in our day-to-day life, but a true "chaotic" system simply means a deterministic system that is extremely sensitive to change. In other words, in a chaotic system with perfectly measured initial conditions, we can accurately predict the final outcome. Conversely, any slight inaccuracy or change in intial condition often leads to exponential differences, and when compounded over a long time leads to seemingly random results.

https://theconversation.com/what-is-chaos-a-complex-systems-scientist-explains-169423: (What is chaos? A complex systems scientist explains)
Chaos theory has a deep connection to physics and climate modeling; there are reasons why we can't predict the weather in a day or a week, but we can be fairly confident about long-term weather patterns, or climate, and chaos theory helps scientists explain what measurements are and aren't reasonable to predict. It's quite a contrast to previous beliefs held by scientists such as Newton who believed that the universe was bound by set rules which would lead to a predictable outcome, but chaos theory demonstrates that compounding effects of tiny errors lead to completely different results.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_UoTTq651dE&t=135s: (Why 5/3 is a fundamental constant for turbulence)
Though turbulence is one of the most famous unsolved questions in classical physics, research done by Andrey Kolmogorov in the 20th century has played a huge role in our understanding. Turbulence in fluids is characterized by eddies, chaos, and diffusion, and though it's still largely unpredictable, Kolmogorov discovered that the energy of a turbulent system contained in eddies of a certain size decreased by an exponent of 5/3, but only remains true in a specific range of eddie diameters. However, this has led to our understanding that there is an underlying predictable nature to some chaotic systems.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9xQehs7fyS8: (🌪️ Turbulence: Chaotic Motion Beyond Prediction)
One of the biggest unsolved questions in physics is why fluids turn to turbulence: specifically, most fluids start out in a predictable, smooth state called laminar flow, but then suddenly switch to the more chaotic turbulent state. The main question is why? Physicists have discovered that turbulence within a fluid corresponds to its Reynolds Number, which is a measurement of the ratio between the fluid's inertia and viscosity. More results from the 20th century also demonstrated a pattern throughout all scales of turbulence, from large air pockets to the blood flow in our bodies, the way energy within the fluid dissipates remains the same. There are also many different methods to analyze and predict turbulence, yet precise calculations are quite expensive; instead, the modern method is to use cheaper approximations, though the use of AI models to synthesize the data and create their own predictions is becoming much more popular.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ovJcsL7vyrk: (This equation will change how you see the world (the logistic map))
A lot of real-world statistics and measurements rely on the simple relation x_(n+1) = rx_n(1-x_n), called the logistic map, to model logistic and population growth. Aside from being quite accurate for predictions, plotting it over long-term potential values reveals a chaotic undercurrent that has periods of stability. There are also many properties that relate this function to the famous Mandelbrot Set, the classic fractal used to illustrate chaos in a diagram. Furthermore, many experiments in both biological and environmental systems were found to have a close relation to this logistic map.

https://schoolcoders.com/data-representation/numbers/binary-shift/: (binary shift, schoolcoders)
Binary shifts are a kind of map within bit manipulation, and fall into three categories: logical shifts left, logical shifts right, and arithmetic shifts. For the first two types a byte of binary data has each valued shifted to the left or right, and the furthest 0 or 1 at the end disappears and is replaced by a 0 at the opposite end. Arithmetic shifts are similar but are used for dealing with signed integers, where the signed term remains unchanged and the rest of the values follow a logical shift pattern.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aAJkLh76QnM: (Chaos | Chapter 7 : Strange Attractors - The butterfly effect)
Lorenz created a extremely simplified model of the atmosphere that utilized a set of three differential equations based on three variables. For example, we could enter in the initial conditions of temperature, wind speed, and humidity, and then plot the vector field of the differential equations as our prediction of future states. Though this system is chaotic and extremely sensitive to changes in these initial conditions, across all potential initial states the vector fields seemed to orbit around certain areas, what we call today as Lorenz or strange attractors. The shape of the paths typically makes a butterfly-like shape around the attractors, a lesser known phenomenon overshadowed by the mainstream butterfly effect.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uDpYU01dhk0: (Chaotic Attractors: a Working Definition of Chaos and Strange Attractors)
Within chaotic systems, physicists have defined more mathematical boundaries to establish what objects are considered strange attractors. The three criterion to determine if something is an attractor are: it must be an invariant set, attracts an open set of initial conditions, and there is no subset of this attractor that satisifies the previous two conditions (basically ensuring minimality). Sometimes an additional condition regarding objects with trajectories in the neighborhood of the attractor have to continue to stay within its locality.

Some famous examples include the Mandelbrot Set, Koch Snowflake, and Serpinski Triangle. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FnRhnZbDprE: (A 1.58-Dimensional Object - Numberphile)
From a more intuitive standpoint, the Hausdorff dimension of an object or shape is closely related to how an object scales in size; there are more mathematical definitions and constraints than this however. For example, when doubling the size of a square we would need to use 4 squares, and thus the dimension of a square is log_2(4) = 2. Similarly, the Serpinski triangle takes 3 copies to double so it has a dimension of approximately 1.59. Using this working defintion many interesting properties arise, such as certain 3-dimensional appearing objects technically being 2-dimensional and many other confusing situations. However, there are many different versions of defining dimensions and it varies based on which property is being measured. Tradtitional notions (called topological) of dimension cannot apply to fractals as they have infinite perimeter.

Gemini explanations on classical methods of counting fractal dimensions:
The box-counting method of dimensions is based on the idea of intersecting grids of shrinking unit sizes with the fractal and measuring the number of boxes that contain some point of the fractal. The way this ratio scales is uniform, which makes the dimension well-defined. Hausdorff/self-similarity mentioned above. Richardson's Ruler utilizes measuring the perimeter of a fractal with different sized rulers; although the perimeter is technically infinite, how it changes exponentially with respect to the ruler's length determines the dimension.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YNIm2Op7UPg&list=PLbN57C5Zdl6j_qJA-pARJnKsmROzPnO9V&index=3: (MAE5790-2 One dimensional Systems), part 2
In dynamical systems sense, bifurcations are when small changes in parameters have a significant effect or change on the stability of a system, typically appearing as altering fixed points. The main three types of bifurcations are: saddle bifurcations, when the number of fixed points change as the parameter does; transcritical bifurcations, or when a fixed point remains even under changes in parameters; and pitchfork bifurcations, when the fixed points are usually preserved (the name comes from the shape of the locus of stable points with respect to the parameter).

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mcqrn9V7_YI&list=PLUeHTafWecAUqSh3Gy0NNr7H3OsXoC-aK&index=5: (Graphical Analysis of 1D Nonlinear ODEs: Fixed Points & Stability (Strogatz Ch. 2))
Continuation on the graphical analysis of 1-dimensional functions: it can be quite hard to observe certain qualities of a system completely through mathematics and using qualitative observations from the graph of a non-linear system can give us plenty of information on things like fixed points and the stability of those points. For example, with 1-dimensional motion we often observe velocity graphs and by simplying observation any intersections with the x-axis we can determine 0-velocity or fixed points (since at these points there is no motion, so a point reaching a fixed point would stay there). Furthermore, we can determine the stability of such a point by looking at its movement based on small perturbations or displacements; when using a linear Taylor series approximation of the velocity over a small displacement, a positive slope implies unstability, whereas a negative slope implies stability.
