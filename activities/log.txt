https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xE7qO6OvKDY: (Chaos Theory Explained in Simple Terms)
This video discusses the basic ideas and foundations of chaos theory and deterministic systems, which are systems in which we use initial conditions and something's current state to accurately predict future states. Chaos theory explores the idea of how within deterministic systems, errors arising from measuring the initial conditions and current state can compound and lead to something that appears as randomness. The classic example is typically characterized by the hypothetical scenario in which a butterfly in Mexico flapping its wings could lead to the formation of a tornado in Texas. 

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VVQY5NNbM3k: (What is Chaos theory? & How its Used Today)
This video defines chaos theory as the study of predicting inherently unpredictable systems, and chaos as the "unpredictibility in the behavior of a complex natural system." Although chaos theory began as a method used to find patterns in the weather and meteorology, its application in a wide range of fields has led to numerous discoveries. For example the walking motion of some biped robots was found to follow a chaotic dynamic, and the process in which some molecules bind when forming synthetic polymers was optimized when chaos theory was applied. 

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5fRhasVmcUE: (Chaos Theory and The Butterfly Effect - Predicting The Unpredictable)
Really great video which clarifies most of the general questions I had about chaos theory and presented by knowledgeable researchers on the topic. "Chaos" and "random" are often used interchangeably in our day-to-day life, but a true "chaotic" system simply means a deterministic system that is extremely sensitive to change. In other words, in a chaotic system with perfectly measured initial conditions, we can accurately predict the final outcome. Conversely, any slight inaccuracy or change in intial condition often leads to exponential differences, and when compounded over a long time leads to seemingly random results.

https://theconversation.com/what-is-chaos-a-complex-systems-scientist-explains-169423: (What is chaos? A complex systems scientist explains)
Chaos theory has a deep connection to physics and climate modeling; there are reasons why we can't predict the weather in a day or a week, but we can be fairly confident about long-term weather patterns, or climate, and chaos theory helps scientists explain what measurements are and aren't reasonable to predict. It's quite a contrast to previous beliefs held by scientists such as Newton who believed that the universe was bound by set rules which would lead to a predictable outcome, but chaos theory demonstrates that compounding effects of tiny errors lead to completely different results.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_UoTTq651dE&t=135s: (Why 5/3 is a fundamental constant for turbulence)
Though turbulence is one of the most famous unsolved questions in classical physics, research done by Andrey Kolmogorov in the 20th century has played a huge role in our understanding. Turbulence in fluids is characterized by eddies, chaos, and diffusion, and though it's still largely unpredictable, Kolmogorov discovered that the energy of a turbulent system contained in eddies of a certain size decreased by an exponent of 5/3, but only remains true in a specific range of eddie diameters. However, this has led to our understanding that there is an underlying predictable nature to some chaotic systems.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9xQehs7fyS8: (🌪️ Turbulence: Chaotic Motion Beyond Prediction)
One of the biggest unsolved questions in physics is why fluids turn to turbulence: specifically, most fluids start out in a predictable, smooth state called laminar flow, but then suddenly switch to the more chaotic turbulent state. The main question is why? Physicists have discovered that turbulence within a fluid corresponds to its Reynolds Number, which is a measurement of the ratio between the fluid's inertia and viscosity. More results from the 20th century also demonstrated a pattern throughout all scales of turbulence, from large air pockets to the blood flow in our bodies, the way energy within the fluid dissipates remains the same. There are also many different methods to analyze and predict turbulence, yet precise calculations are quite expensive; instead, the modern method is to use cheaper approximations, though the use of AI models to synthesize the data and create their own predictions is becoming much more popular.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ovJcsL7vyrk: (This equation will change how you see the world (the logistic map))
A lot of real-world statistics and measurements rely on the simple relation x_(n+1) = rx_n(1-x_n), called the logistic map, to model logistic and population growth. Aside from being quite accurate for predictions, plotting it over long-term potential values reveals a chaotic undercurrent that has periods of stability. There are also many properties that relate this function to the famous Mandelbrot Set, the classic fractal used to illustrate chaos in a diagram. Furthermore, many experiments in both biological and environmental systems were found to have a close relation to this logistic map.
